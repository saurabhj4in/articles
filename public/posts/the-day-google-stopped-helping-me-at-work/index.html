The Day Google Stopped Helping Me at Work

When you get promoted, you stop fixing bugs and start choosing battles. No StackOverflow for that.

Saurabh Jain
January 23, 2026 · 2 min read · View on Medium ↗
Originally published on Medium — read there for comments & claps →

You Don’t Fix Bugs Anymore. You Choose Battles.

When you first enter cybersecurity, the work looks simple. Find issue. Fix issue. Move on. It mirrors 8-bit logic — bad actors emerge, you respond with solutions, you advance.

Then you get promoted… and things get weird.

You transition from fixing problems to deciding which problems deserve to be fixed. Your new daily responsibilities:

  1. The Existential Crisis — “This vulnerability exists… but should we really fix it?”
  2. The Gambler’s Itch — “It’s risky, but I’m feeling lucky today.”
  3. The Stability Fear — Worrying that security fixes might destabilize production systems

Technical Skills? It’s Judgment Time Now.

At senior levels, the role shifts from technical execution to strategic decision-making. You navigate questions like:

  • Do we accept this risk or escalate it?
  • Do I challenge leadership even if it creates friction?
  • Do I address something preemptively or wait for incidents?

No online resource has answers. There’s no StackOverflow thread for “should I push back on the VP about this auth bypass during a product freeze.” Only anxiety and responsibility.

Security is 50 Shades of Grey

Security decisions rarely have a clear right or wrong answer. Consider this scenario: A vulnerability exists with low exploitation probability but high business impact, and substantial remediation costs.

Do you:

  • Fix it and risk destabilizing production?
  • Ignore it and hope no one finds it first?
  • Document risk acceptance that nobody reads?

The field becomes political, strategic, and fundamentally human — balancing panicked stakeholders, security-averse developers, and budget-conscious management.

Welcome to Senior Mode

Uncertainty isn’t inexperience. It’s the sign that you understand real consequences.

Junior engineers ask: “Did I miss an alert?”
Senior engineers ask: “Did I just create a future apology post scenario?”

The weight you feel is responsibility. Your decisions protect businesses, customers, reputations, and careers. One miscalculation triggers:

  • Social media uproar
  • Executive scrutiny
  • Your name in the incident report

The Takeaway

Feeling uncertain doesn’t mean you’re inexperienced. It means you’re finally making decisions that matter.

That heaviness is growth. Embrace it — or at least stop Googling your way out of it.
