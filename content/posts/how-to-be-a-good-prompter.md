It's nearly midnight before a deadline. You open ChatGPT, type "write an email to my boss," and hit enter.

What comes back is a masterpiece of corporate formality — sophisticated vocabulary, elaborate structure, tone of a Victorian diplomat. You spend 40 minutes stripping it down to something you'd actually send.

That's not an AI problem. That's a prompting problem.

## The Core Rule

**The more you tell, the better you get.**

Prompt engineering isn't an art or a talent. It's closer to mathematics: input quality determines output quality. Vague in, vague out. Specific in, useful out.

## The Subway Analogy

If you walk into a Subway and say "make me something," you might get a random combination that's technically food but not what you wanted.

If you say "toasted Italian bread, double cheese, turkey, no olives, extra southwest sauce" — you get exactly what you wanted.

AI works the same way. It wants to help. It just needs to know what "help" looks like to you.

## The Three Things Every Good Prompt Has

### 1. Context

Who are you? What are you working on? What's the situation?

**Weak:** "Write an email to my boss."  
**Strong:** "Write an email to my manager explaining that the security audit will take two more days than planned. Tone: professional but direct. I want to reassure them without over-promising."

### 2. Format

What do you want back? A paragraph? A list? A table? Code? A first draft you'll edit?

**Weak:** "Explain zero trust."  
**Strong:** "Explain zero trust in 3 bullet points suitable for a slide deck aimed at a non-technical executive audience."

### 3. Constraints

What are the boundaries? Length, tone, things to avoid, things to include?

**Weak:** "Give me some security tips."  
**Strong:** "Give me 5 practical security tips for a 10-person startup. No enterprise tools, no expensive solutions. Each tip in one sentence with a specific action."

## Common Prompting Mistakes

**Treating AI like a search engine.** Single keywords return generic results. AI works better with full sentences and context.

**Asking for too much at once.** "Write a complete security policy, incident response plan, and employee training curriculum" is too much in one go. Break it into steps.

**Not iterating.** The first response is rarely the best. Follow up: "Make it shorter," "Change the tone to be more casual," "Add a section on X," "Remove the jargon."

**Forgetting to specify the audience.** The same concept explained for a developer vs. a CEO vs. a journalist should read completely differently. Tell the AI who it's talking to.

## A Template to Start With

```
Role: [Who you are / what role you want the AI to play]
Task: [What you want done, specifically]
Audience: [Who will read or use this]
Format: [What the output should look like]
Constraints: [Length, tone, what to include/exclude]
Context: [Any background the AI needs]
```

You don't need all six every time. But the more you fill in, the less work you'll do cleaning up the output.

## The Mindset Shift

Stop thinking of prompts as commands and start thinking of them as briefs.

You wouldn't hand a new colleague a one-line task and expect them to read your mind. You'd explain the context, the goal, the audience, and what done looks like.

AI is the same. Brief it properly, and it becomes genuinely useful. Brief it poorly, and you'll spend more time editing than you would have spent just writing it yourself.
